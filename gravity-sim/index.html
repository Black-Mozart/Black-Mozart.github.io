§ 01

Newton's Law of Gravity

Foundation

Every mass attracts every other mass. Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation gives the force as proportional to both masses and inversely proportional to the square of their separation.

Universal Gravitation
$$F = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$
Gravitational acceleration on body i from all others
$$\vec{a}_i = G\sum_{\substack{j=1\\j\neq i}}^{N}\frac{m_j(\vec{r}_j-\vec{r}_i)}{|\vec{r}_j-\vec{r}_i|^3}$$

This engine uses $G = 60{,}000$ simulation units. The inverse-square falloff creates the deep funnel shape you see in the curvature mesh — force drops rapidly with distance.

📐

Doubling the distance between two bodies reduces the gravitational force by four times — this is why orbits are stable. At just the right distance the centripetal acceleration exactly balances the gravitational pull.

§ 02

Space-Time Curvature Mesh

Einstein's Rubber Sheet

General Relativity tells us mass curves space-time. The rubber-sheet visualises this: each grid point's vertical displacement represents the depth of the gravitational potential well at that location.

Newtonian gravitational potential at position r
$$\Phi(\vec{r}) = -G\sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{m_i}{|\vec{r}-\vec{r}_i|}$$
Visual mesh displacement at grid point (gx, gz)
$$\delta y(g_x,g_z) = -G_{vis}\sum_{i}\frac{m_i}{\sqrt{(g_x-x_i)^2+(g_z-z_i)^2+\varepsilon^2}^{\,2}+\delta}$$

§ 03

Gravitational Softening

Stability

At very close range, $r \to 0$ makes force blow up to infinity causing numerical explosions. A softening parameter ε (Plummer softening) prevents this.

Standard — singular at r = 0
$$F = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}$$
Plummer softened — stable for all r
$$F = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2+\varepsilon^2}$$
Plummer potential
$$\Phi(r) = -\frac{Gm}{\sqrt{r^2+\varepsilon^2}}$$
⚠

This engine uses two softening values: ε = 18 px for body physics and ε = 30 px for the visual mesh. The larger visual value keeps the well shape smooth. For $r \gg \varepsilon$ the force converges to exact Newtonian.

§ 04

Velocity Verlet Integration

2nd-Order Symplectic

To advance positions and velocities in time we use Velocity Verlet — a 2nd-order symplectic integrator. Unlike simple Euler, it averages old and new accelerations, making it energy-conserving and time-reversible.

Step 1 — position update
$$\vec{r}(t+h)=\vec{r}(t)+\vec{v}(t)\cdot h+\tfrac{1}{2}\vec{a}(t)\cdot h^2$$
Step 2 — recompute acceleration at new positions
$$\vec{a}(t+h)=\sum_j\text{GravForce}\bigl(\vec{r}(t+h)\bigr)$$
Step 3 — velocity correction using average acceleration
$$\vec{v}(t+h)=\vec{v}(t)+\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[\vec{a}(t)+\vec{a}(t+h)\bigr]\cdot h$$
🔁

Physics runs in the XZ plane only. The Y axis is purely visual — the mesh deformation. Gravity never actually pulls bodies "down"; it acts horizontally, and the grid visualises the potential field separately.

§ 05

Geodesics & Test Particles

General Relativity

In GR, free-falling objects follow geodesics — straightest paths through curved space-time. Locally they feel no force; globally they curve around masses. Press T to shoot a massless test particle along a geodesic.

Geodesic equation (Newtonian approximation)
$$\frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2}=-\nabla\Phi(\vec{r})=G\sum_i\frac{m_i(\vec{r}_i-\vec{r})}{|\vec{r}_i-\vec{r}|^3}$$
Light deflection — Schwarzschild metric (exact GR)
$$\delta\phi=\frac{4GM}{c^2 b}$$
$b$ = impact parameter · first confirmed by Eddington (1919 solar eclipse)
✦

Test particles here have zero mass — they don't affect the field. They integrate gravitational acceleration at 350 px/s initial speed, tracing the curved paths that photons and small objects follow through the simulated space-time.

§ 06

Gravitational Waves

Radial Propagation

Body mergers emit gravitational waves — ripples in space-time propagating outward. This engine simulates them as expanding rings with oscillating mesh displacement and exponential amplitude falloff.

Wave front expansion
$$R(t)=R_0+v_{\text{wave}}\cdot t\qquad v_{\text{wave}}=260\text{ px/s}$$
Mesh displacement from wave at distance d
$$\delta y_w=A\cdot\cos\!\bigl(|d-R|\cdot k\bigr)\cdot e^{-|d-R|\cdot\gamma}\cdot\mathcal{L}(t)$$
🌊

LIGO detected the first gravitational wave in 2015 — from two merging black holes 1.3 billion light-years away. The strain was $h\approx10^{-21}$, smaller than 1/1000th the width of a proton. Real waves have "plus" and "cross" polarizations that stretch/compress space perpendicularly.

§ 07

3D Perspective Projection

Rendering Math

The mesh lives in 3D world space (XYZ) but must render on a 2D canvas. We apply two rotation matrices then a perspective divide.

Rotate around Y axis (horizontal pan, θ_Y = 0.18 rad)
$$\begin{pmatrix}r_x\\r_{z_0}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta_Y&\sin\theta_Y\\-\sin\theta_Y&\cos\theta_Y\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}w_x\\w_z\end{pmatrix}$$
Rotate around X axis (tilt down, θ_X = 0.70 rad ≈ 40°)
$$r_y=w_y\cos\theta_X-r_{z_0}\sin\theta_X\qquad r_z=w_y\sin\theta_X+r_{z_0}\cos\theta_X$$
Perspective divide (focal f = 560, camera offset d₀ = 680)
$$s_x=c_x+\frac{r_x\cdot f}{r_z+d_0}\qquad s_y=c_y+\frac{r_y\cdot f}{r_z+d_0}$$

Pseudocode and complexity analysis for every system in the Gravity Field Simulator.

Grid Deformation O(N·G²) per frame
// 38×38 = 1444 grid points, recomputed every frame
function updateGrid(bodies, gridPoints):
  for each point g:
    dy = 0
    for each body b:
      dist = sqrt((g.wx-b.wx)² + (g.wz-b.wz)² + ε_vis²)
      dy  -= G_vis * b.mass / (dist² + δ)
    if showWaves:
      for each wave w:
        d_to_front = |distance(g, w.origin) - w.radius|
        if d_to_front < 30:
          dy += cos(d*k) * exp(-d*γ) * w.life * w.strength * 8
    g.wy = clamp(dy, -MAX_DEPTH, 20)
Velocity Verlet N-body O(N²) per step
function verletStep(dt):
  // Step 1 — advance positions
  for i: pos[i] += vel[i]*dt + 0.5*acc[i]*dt²

  // Step 2 — new accelerations at updated positions
  for i:
    newAcc[i] = 0
    for j≠i:
      d² = (pos[j]-pos[i])² + ε²
      newAcc[i] += G * mass[j] / d² * direction(i→j)

  // Step 3 — average acceleration velocity correction
  for i:
    vel[i] += 0.5 * (acc[i] + newAcc[i]) * dt
    acc[i]  = newAcc[i]
Exact Screen→World Inverse Analytical
// Exact inverse of the 3D projection for y=0 (grid plane)
// Used for spawn placement and pan
function screenToWorldFlat(sx, sy):
  dsy  = sy - screenCentreY
  // Solve for rz0 analytically (from y=0 constraint)
  denom = dsy * cos(θX) / FOCAL + sin(θX)
  rz0   = -(dsy * CAM_OFFSET / FOCAL) / denom
  depth = rz0 * cos(θX) + CAM_OFFSET
  rx    = (sx - screenCentreX) * depth / FOCAL
  // Inverse Y rotation
  tx = rx * cos(θY) - rz0 * sin(θY)
  tz = rx * sin(θY) + rz0 * cos(θY)
  return { wx: tx/zoom + offX, wz: tz/zoom + offY }
